You're right to be suspicious—the math in that chart is definitely "hallucinating."



Looking at your numbers, the chart shows a Balance of $560,000 but only $4,099 in Total Interest. On a standard loan, that interest amount is incredibly low (less than 1% total).



### The Mathematical Disconnect



If you are paying off $560k and only accruing $4k in interest over ~2.5 years (Apr '26 to Aug '28), the visual "slope" of the Cumulative Interest (the yellow line/area) should be almost flat against the bottom of the graph.



Current Issues:



* Scale: The green balance line drops to $0, but the interest remains a tiny fraction. On a typical high-interest debt, that yellow area would take up a much larger chunk of the graph.
* The "Vs. Balance" Calculation: As we discussed, 1¢ per $1 is very low. Most users with a "Business Credit Card" (as seen at the top) are likely paying 15–25% APR. If this is a real-world scenario, that interest figure ($4,099) is likely missing a zero or two, which is why the chart looks "off."



---



### How to Fix the Chart Logic



To make the chart accurate and meaningful for a user, the visualization needs to reflect the Cost of Delay.



Feature Fix
Y-Axis Should represent the Total Debt Burden (Principal + Interest).
The Slope The green line should only hit $0 when the total interest is also paid.
The "What If" Sync When a user types "$500" into that extra payment box, the green line should visibly "steepen" and the end date (Aug '28) should pull back to an earlier date.
### Better Visualization Idea: "The Gap"



Instead of two lines that are hard to compare, use a Stacked Area Chart.



* Bottom Layer (Green): The Principal ($560,000).
* Top Layer (Orange/Red): The Interest ($4,099+).
* The Goal: The user wants to "shrink" the top layer by paying extra.



---



### Recommendation for the "1¢ per $1" Box



Since the math is confusing and the chart feels inaccurate, I suggest changing that specific data point to Interest Saved.



If they add $100/mo, show them: "You save $X,XXX in interest." That is a much stronger psychological driver than a "per dollar" ratio.



Would you like me to draft a logic flow for how the chart should update when a user enters an "Extra Payment" amount?

You’ve spotted a major logical flaw. If every strategy shows the exact same Payoff Time (1 yr 11 mo) and Total Interest ($4,009), the "Strategy" page loses its entire purpose.



The Avalanche method is mathematically designed to save more money than Snowball, so the numbers should be different. If they aren't, the user will feel like the "Recommended" tag is arbitrary or that the app is broken.



### Why the math looks "frozen"



It is highly likely that with only one debt entered, the math is identical across all strategies. Strategy only matters when you have 2+ debts to prioritize.



---



### How to fix this UX "Red Flag"



#### 1. Add a "Conditional State"



If the user only has one debt, don't show the Strategy comparison yet. Instead, show a message:



"Add another debt to see which strategy saves you the most money."



#### 2. Fix the "Best" Logic



* Avalanche should almost always show the lowest "Total Interest Paid."
* Snowball should often show the fastest "First Debt Paid" date.
* Recommended: Only tag a strategy as "Recommended" if it actually provides a unique benefit (e.g., "Saves you $1,200 in interest" or "First win in 3 months").



#### 3. Visual Differentiation



If the numbers are the same, the user has no reason to click. You need to show the contrast:



Strategy Payoff Time Total Interest Benefit Tag
Snowball 2 yrs 1 mo $5,200 Fastest Win
Avalanche 1 yr 11 mo $4,009 Cheapest Plan
